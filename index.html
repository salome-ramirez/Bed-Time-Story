JMM 629 Final Project

Bed Time
Story

Sleep is essential at every age. This chronicle details exactly how much sleep Americans actually get and the toll it takes on their bodies and minds from age six to eighty-five.

scroll to begin reading ↓

S

leep is one of the few things the body cannot do without. The hours we spend in it are when growth happens, when memories are filed, when the immune system is rebuilt, when the brain clears the day's accumulated waste. None of that is optional. And yet at every age, on every night, most Americans are short.

This piece walks through five life stages (childhood, adolescence, young adulthood, adulthood, and older age) to ask two consistent questions of each: How much do they sleep? And what suffers when they fall short?

I

ages 6 to 12

Children

Most American children still meet their baseline sleep needs. Those who fall short reveal the costs in diminished attention spans, excess body weight, and more days lost to sickness.

57%

of American children aged 6 to 12 meet the recommended 9 to 12 hours of sleep on weeknights.
National Survey of Children's Health, 2020.

When the lights go out, the pituitary gland goes to work. The deepest stages of childhood sleep are when growth hormone is released, when memories from the day are filed, when the immune system is rebuilt. Most of the body's construction happens after dark.

The American Academy of Sleep Medicine recommends nine to twelve hours each night for children aged six to twelve. A little over half of American children get there.

The shape of the orbit immediately reveals the finding. Most children cluster close to the moon, nearing the recommended hours. Yet the outer rings are not empty. A meaningful share of American children sleep seven hours, six hours, or even less on school nights. These are the children the rest of this story will follow.

The mental cost of short sleep at this age is paid in the classroom. Attention spans collapse. Behavioral problems rise. Memory consolidation, which depends on the deepest stages of the night, never quite finishes its work.

This is the baseline. The night a child does not get is the night the body cannot replace. And those who miss it, that smaller, harder-to-see group, are the ones whose deficit will compound for the rest of this story.

II

ages 13 to 17

Teenagers

Sleep collapses during adolescence. This drop is the steepest in the human lifespan, tracking closely alongside rising teenage anxiety.

77%

of American high schoolers sleep less than 8 hours on a school night, the recommended minimum for their age.
CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey, 2021.

At puberty, two things happen at once. The body's circadian clock shifts later by about two hours. And the school day, in most American districts, begins before 8 a.m. The body and the bell are out of phase.

The result is the steepest sleep collapse in the human lifespan. American children average just under ten hours a night. American teenagers average under seven. The drop happens in the space of one or two grade levels.

The childhood orbit, plotted here on the same scale, now resembles an entirely different population. The dense central cluster has migrated outward. The well-rested group, visible and large in childhood, has nearly disappeared.

Anxiety scores rise in lockstep with the loss of hours. Persistent sadness, suicidality, and academic struggle all track the same way. The teenage mental-health crisis American media has spent the last decade naming is, in part, a sleep crisis.

This is the steepest collapse in the human lifespan. From here forward, no chapter will show the body returning to its childhood baseline.

III

ages 18 to 25

Young Adult

Young adults sleep less than the recommended minimum on most weeknights. The body keeps the record as burnout scores climb in lockstep with the lost hours.

35%

of American adults aged 18 to 25 report fewer than 7 hours of sleep on a typical night.
Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System, 2022.

By their twenties, most Americans are sleeping less than the eight hours their bodies still need. They have learned to call six and a half hours a normal night. The body, however, has not.

The chapter's central finding is the relationship between those missing hours and burnout. As nightly sleep falls below seven hours, burnout scores rise sharply. As it falls below six, the curve goes vertical.

Burnout is not a failure of willpower. It is a biological sleep-debt arithmetic the calendar continuously runs. The body's stress response, naturally calibrated for occasional emergencies, now triggers every weekday morning, and the morning cortisol numbers prove it. Below, the rising stress hormone closely tracks the widening weekday-to-weekend sleep gap.

By the time American sleepers reach the end of their twenties, they largely stop registering the deficit as a problem. The body has fully adapted to chronic insufficiency rather than sufficient rest. The next chapter explores what happens when that adaptation becomes a permanent way of life.

IV

ages 26 to 64

Adult

For four decades, the average American sleeps under seven hours a night. The body keeps a strict record through rising blood pressure and a lengthening list of chronic conditions.

6.9h

average sleep for American adults 26 to 64. The recommended minimum is 7 hours.
National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey.

By middle age, insufficient sleep has settled into a plateau. The total duration stays roughly constant for forty years at just under seven hours a night, slightly above what young adults reported. But the underlying architecture of that sleep has fundamentally changed.

The body begins waking itself up at night. Sometimes outside causes like a child, a sudden noise, or an alarm trigger this. Often, the causes are internal: an early spike in stress hormones, a protesting digestive system, or a thought refusing to settle. The total hours might look reasonable, but the architecture beneath them does not.

Stress and depression both follow the curve of lost hours. The body that sleeps less is the body that copes worse. The body that copes worse is the body that sleeps less. It is the most familiar feedback loop in adult health.

This plateau is not truly stable. It is a long, quiet stretch during which the bill finally comes due in the form of cardiovascular risk, metabolic dysregulation, and the slow dimming of attention. Four decades of insufficient sleep provides enough time for the body's small accommodations to permanently alter its shape.

V

age 65 and beyond

Senior

Older Americans do not sleep significantly less overall, but they do sleep in pieces. The night that was once a single block shatters into many short fragments, exacting a heavy toll on the brain.

40%

of Americans over 65 wake two or more times on a typical night. The body has trouble holding sleep together.
National Health and Aging Trends Study.

In the long evening of life, the total time spent in bed often looks healthy on paper. Older adults spend nearly as much time in bed as younger adults do. However, their sleep efficiency (the actual fraction of that time spent asleep) begins to steadily fall.

The night fragments. Each interruption is short, perhaps lasting only a few minutes, and the sleeper rarely remembers it by morning. However, the brain's nightly cleaning cycles completely depend on uninterrupted deep sleep, meaning they cannot finish what they started.

The link between sleep and cognition becomes one of the most consequential variables in late-life health. It is also highly modifiable, relying less on changing the total hours spent in bed and more on aggressively protecting sleep continuity.

The architecture is not destroyed in this chapter. It has been wearing away, decade by decade, for years. By now what remains is a night of fragments, each too short to do the body's work.

A note on the data

The 7,500 lives in this piece are simulated. Each chapter's distributions are calibrated to published findings from federal health surveys: the National Survey of Children's Health (children), the CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey (adolescents), the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (young adults), the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (adults), and the National Health and Aging Trends Study (older adults). The simulated rows let the piece visualize relationships at the scale of individual stars while preserving the population-level shapes those surveys report.

Within-group relationships, such as the slope of attention against sleep duration in children, are calibrated to peer-reviewed effect sizes from published medical literature. Cumulative deficit calculations in the closing interactive use the recommended minimum hours for each age band (10 for children, 9 for adolescents, 8 for young adults, 7.5 for adults and older adults) and the group's average actual sleep, multiplied by the years a person has lived in that band.

Numbers should be read as illustrative rather than diagnostic. Real human sleep varies widely from the simulated averages, and individual circumstances can produce very different trajectories.
