A data story about sleep in America

Bed Time Story

One in three Americans doesn't get enough sleep. Across every stage of life, the cost is being paid — in classrooms, in clinics, and in years.

Children

Sleep sufficiency across all age groups

Children ages 6 to 12 need between 9 and 12 hours of sleep per night. Yet a substantial portion fall short — and the consequences show up not just in the morning, but in the classroom and the doctor's office.

How many children meet sleep guidelines?

Short sleep in children doesn't just cause tiredness. It's strongly associated with behavioral and attention disorders — conditions that affect learning and development at a critical window.

Short sleep and behavioral health conditions

The physical effects compound over time. Children who consistently sleep less show higher rates of obesity, and the relationship holds even when controlling for physical activity levels.

Sleep duration and weight status

No age group is losing more sleep than teenagers. Biological clock shifts, school start times, and screens have converged to produce a generation running on chronic deficit. The data is stark.

Teen sleep hours, 2013–2023

The mental health toll is the most dramatic of any age group. Teens who sleep fewer than 8 hours report feelings of sadness and hopelessness at nearly twice the rate of those meeting guidelines.

Mental health indicators among high school students

The decline has not been equal. Boys started higher in 2013 and have fallen further. Girls were already sleeping less and have continued to drop — both now well below the 8-hour guideline.

% getting sufficient sleep by sex, 2013 vs 2023

Physical activity also suffers — a feedback loop where tired teens exercise less, which makes sleep quality worse, which makes them more tired.

Physical activity rates by sleep sufficiency

Young adults face a transition: the mental health consequences of adolescent sleep deprivation don't disappear — they're joined by the first signs of physical consequences. Work, college, and social life keep the sleep debt growing.

Short sleep prevalence, ages 18–34

Depression rates among chronically sleep-deprived young adults are significantly elevated. This age group also shows the sharpest response to even modest improvements in sleep duration.

Depression prevalence by sleep hours

Physical health ratings begin to diverge. Young adults sleeping fewer than 6 hours are significantly more likely to rate their own health as fair or poor — a self-assessment that predicts longer-term outcomes.

Self-rated health by sleep category

By middle age, sleep deprivation has had years to accumulate. The consequences shift from behavioral and mood-based to cardiovascular and metabolic — measurable in bloodwork and medical records.

Sleep duration across the adult lifespan

Depression remains a significant correlate, but the strongest mental health signal at this stage is the compounding effect — adults who have been sleep-deprived since their twenties show persistently elevated risk.

Depression prevalence by sleep category

Cardiovascular markers tell the clearest story. Blood pressure and blood sugar both show dose-response relationships with sleep duration — more deprivation, worse numbers.

Blood pressure and HbA1c by sleep hours

Sleep and health outcomes — heatmap

Among adults 65 and older, the sleep story shifts again. Total sleep time often increases — but quality degrades. Sleep disorders become common. And the consequences now include cognitive decline.

Sleep sufficiency rates, 65+

Cognitive concern and depression rates among older adults track closely with sleep quality. The relationship between sleep disruption and dementia risk has become one of the most active areas of sleep research.

Cognitive concern by sleep sufficiency

Physical health markers among older poor sleepers show elevated rates of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and obesity — the accumulated cost of a lifetime of insufficient sleep.

Chronic condition prevalence by sleep category

Sleep is not a luxury. It's infrastructure.

Across every stage of life — from the developing brain of a six-year-old to the aging mind of a seventy-year-old — sleep deprivation leaves a measurable mark. The consequences are not abstract. They show up in classrooms, in emergency rooms, in blood pressure readings, and in the gradual erosion of memory and cognition that comes with age.

The data collected tell a consistent story: we are a chronically under-slept country, and the cost compounds across a lifetime. Children who sleep less struggle more in school. Teenagers carry mental health burdens that follow them into adulthood. Working-age adults develop cardiovascular and metabolic conditions at measurably higher rates. And older adults face an accelerated risk of cognitive decline.

The good news is that sleep is modifiable. Unlike many health risk factors, it responds to behavioral change, policy reform, and public awareness. The first step is understanding the scale of the problem — which is what this data attempts to show.
